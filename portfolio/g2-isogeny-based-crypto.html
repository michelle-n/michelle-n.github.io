Project Details of Genus 2 Isogeny-Based Cryptography

Abstract

Advances in quantum computing have prompted the need to search for and develop new cryptographic protocols that will be secure against quantum attacks. Because a quantum computer capable of implementing Shor’s algorithm would break most current public key cryptosystems, isogeny-based cryptography has been investigated as a setting to develop quantum-resistant cryptographic protocols. While much work in this area revolves around Supersingular Isogeny Diffie-Hellman key-exchange (SIDH) using elliptic curves, less has been published on generalizations to dimension, or genus, two. Nevertheless, this area presents promising returns in terms of smaller key sizes compared to the dimension one protocol, as well as compared to other non-isogeny-based post-quantum schemes. In this work, Flynn and Ti’s genus two SIDH-like key exchange is examined; in particular, the state-of-the-art is surveyed for computing the codomains of isogenies in genus two, such that improvements for the computations in Flynn and Ti’s protocol can be identified.

During my honors research project, I worked under the supervision of Dr. Michael J. Jacobson, Jr. (Cryptography and Computational Number Theory) at the University of Calgary to examine a genus 2 Supersingular Isogeny Diffie-Hellman key-exchange (SIDH) protocol. My key contributions were:

  • Investigating optimizations to genus 2 SIDH, by analyzing over 50 publications, to recover two alternative algorithms.
  • Progressing experiments to compare and enhance the run-time of computations, by initiating the development of Magma code implementing the studied cryptographic protocol and its two alternative algorithms.
